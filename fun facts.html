Fun Facts About Sharks




    Sharks Don't Have Bones

Sharks do not actually have the hard bones that are common in most vertebrates. Instead, they have cartilage, which makes them cartilaginous fish. Cartilage is a soft and flexible connective tissue that gives the sharks their shape as well as protect their organs.





    Some Sharks Glow in the Dark

Scientists working in the eastern coast of New Zealand have discovered three deep-sea shark species that they found to be bioluminescent, which means that they glow in the dark! Bioluminescence in sharks are acheived through the light-producing cells, or photophores found within the shark's skin.





    Sharks Have Electroreception

Electroreception, also referred to as the shark's sixth sense, allows them to detect the electrical currents of other organisms. They can use this sense as a survival tool, for it allows them to find prey hiding in rocks, corals, and even sand.





    Hammerhead Sharks Have Binocular Vision

A hammerhead shark's flattened, hammer-shaped front allows their eyes to be positioned at either side of its head, which gives it 360-degree vision. This makes them have excellect vision and depth perception.





    The Dwarf Lantern Shark is the Smallest Shark

The dwarf lantern shark is a deepsea bioluminescent creature. It is considered the smallest shark in the world and is measured on average as smaller than a human hand. It can reach a maximum size of 20 centimeters.





    Whale Shark Spots Are Unique

Similar to human fingerprints, the spot pattern on the backs of whale sharks are unique to every whale shark. This can help individual sharks to be identified.





    Sharks Can Only Swim Forwards

Due to the inability of sharks' pectoral fins to curve upwards, they have limited capabilities to swim backwards. This makes sharks the only fish that cannot swim backwards.





    The Megalodon

The Megalodon is the largest shark, as well as one of the largest fish, to have inhabited the Earth's ocean. It is an extict species of mackerel sharks. It is estimated to have grown between 15-18 metres in length.
